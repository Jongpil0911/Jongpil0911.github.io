## Abstract

Digital Holographic Microscopy (DHM) is a powerful technique for acquiring three-dimensional (3D) profiles of microscopic objects. <br/>
However, its performance is significantly degraded in low-power laser environments, where noise and low signal-to-noise ratios obscure object details. <br/>
This study introduces a Frame-Stacking Method (FSM) designed to overcome these limitations. <br/>
By capturing and processing multiple frames from a video stream of the object, the FSM enhances the hologram quality, reduces noise, and enables the accurate reconstruction of 3D profiles even under challenging low-light conditions. <br/>
We demonstrate the effectiveness of the FSM through experiments on microspheres and red blood cells (RBCs), showing significant improvements in quantitative metrics such as Mean Squared Error (MSE), Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), and Structural Similarity Index (SSIM).

## 1. Introduction

In Digital Holographic Microscopy (DHM), the 3D profile of a specimen is reconstructed from a recorded hologram. <br/>
The quality of this reconstruction is highly dependent on the power of the laser source. <br/>
As shown in Figure 1, a 3D profile obtained in a normal-power environment (a) is clear and well-defined, while a profile from a low-power environment (b) is dominated by noise, rendering the object unrecognizable. <br/>

![Comparison of the Hologram and Dark Hologram](/assets/img/FSM/compare.png)
*Figure 1: Comparison of 3D profiles and cross-sectional height profiles. (a) Normal-power environment. (b) Low-power laser environment.*

The degradation in quality, particularly the loss of recognizable features like the elliptical shape of microspheres in the cross-sectional profile, necessitates a method to improve accuracy in low-power settings. <br/>
The Frame-Stacking Method (FSM) is proposed to address this challenge.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1. The Frame-Stacking Method (FSM)

The core of the FSM is the modification of the data acquisition and processing pipeline, as illustrated in the flowchart in Figure 2.

![Compare the flowchart of the Hologram image processing and Dark Hologram image processing](/assets/img/FSM/flowchart.png)
*Figure 2: Flowchart comparing the conventional DHM process with the proposed Frame-Stacking Method (FSM).*

Unlike conventional methods that capture a single static image, the FSM involves the following steps:
1.  **Video Acquisition:** A video of the object is recorded instead of a single image.
2.  **Frame Decomposition:** The video is decomposed into individual frames.
3.  **Image Superimposition and Rescaling:** The extracted frames are superimposed and rescaled to create an enhanced hologram.

For the reference hologram, a single image is captured without the object, using normal laser power, as the absence of a specimen makes it unnecessary to use a low-power source. <br/>
This composite approach allows for the reconstruction of a high-accuracy 3D image.

### 2.2. Mathematical Formulation

The enhanced hologram, $I_{Ho-FSM}(x, y)$, is generated by averaging multiple frames, which effectively increases the signal-to-noise ratio. <br/>
The process is defined by the following equations:

$I_{Ho-FSM}(x, y) = \frac{1}{\alpha} \sum^{N}_{i = 1}I_i^{HoD}(x, y)$

$\alpha = \frac{\max \left \{ \sum^{N}_{i = 1} I_i^{HoD} (x, y) \right \}}{255}$

Here, $I_i^{HoD}(x, y)$ is the *i*-th frame captured in the low-power environment, $N$ is the total number of frames, and $\alpha$ is a scaling factor that normalizes the superimposed luminance values to an 8-bit range. <br/>
This process corrects for low object information and compresses random noise, thereby improving the final 3D reconstruction.

### 3.1. Quantitative Comparison: Microspheres

The FSM was first tested on microspheres. <br/>
The results, summarized in Table 1, show a significant improvement in all quantitative metrics compared to the original, unprocessed low-power hologram.

### 3.2. Application to Red Blood Cells (RBCs)

To validate the FSM for biological applications, experiments were conducted using red blood cells (RBCs). <br/>

---

## 4. Conclusion

The Frame-Stacking Method (FSM) provides a robust solution for acquiring high-quality 3D profiles in low-power Digital Holographic Microscopy. <br/>
By integrating information from multiple frames, the FSM effectively mitigates the impact of noise and enhances the signal, enabling accurate 3D reconstruction of both standard microspheres and biological samples like red blood cells. <br/>
The quantitative and qualitative improvements demonstrate that the FSM is a valuable technique for DHM applications where high laser power is not feasible. <br/>
